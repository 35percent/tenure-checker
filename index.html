S106 Tenure Checker

Is your housing association charging too much rent?

Why this Tenure Checker?

Housing associations are taking advantage of the growing confusion surrounding affordable housing tenures and charging higher rents than they should.
The Mayor's monitoring procedures are inadequate because they only involve spot checks, which only check rent levels of the first letting and there is no penalty where housing associations are found charging higher rents than they should.


Over 400,000 affordable homes in London are now managed by housing associations - nearly twice as many as twenty years ago. This is a result of the increasing role that section 106 provision is playing, as well as Mayoral funding which funds around 10,000 affordable homes every year. These Mayoral funding programmes have created confusion because they have created numerous different types of affordable housing rental tenures:

  • Social Rent (set according to the National Rent Regime and pegged to average local incomes - currently around £122 pw in London)
  • London Affordable Rent (capped at benchmark levels set by the Mayor, currently around 30% higher than social rent)
  • Capped Affordable Rent (capped at 50% market rent)
  • London Living Rent (set at one third of average gross local earnings. On average, across London, they are about 67% of market rents)
  • Affordable Rent (up to 80% market rent)

The level of grant is based largely on the affordability of the tenure - i.e. the highest level of grant is available on the most affordable of the tenures. The problem is that once a housing association has received funding and built the homes there is very little in the way of controls or monitoring to ensure that they don't charge higher rents than they should. This is because neither the Mayor nor local authorities keep a full register of the affordable homes built with grant funding or delivered by developers under section 106 agreements.

The Project

The S106 Tenure Checker will be a first step towards making housing associations in London operate in a fairer and more transparent way and ensure that the rents they charge are in line with the tenures agreed at planning approval or by funding agreements. There are three parts to the project:

  • The Register - build a public register of all the (xxx,000) affordable homes secured via section 106 agreements in the past ten years and map them.
  • The Lookup Tool - an online web service that will allow tenants to look up their address, find out if their home is subject to a section 106 agreement and discover if the rent charged by the housing association is higher than it should be.
  • The Reporting Tool – a facility to enable tenants to seek advice or make a complaint about the housing association to their local authority/GLA if the rent charged is higher than the level required by the section 106 agreement.

How can we build the register?

  • Lobbying the Mayor and London Assembly members to work with housing associations
  • Approaching and working with progressive Councils and housing associations - see for example this joint project between Southwark Council and Peabody, which is planning to publish address-level data of affordable homes.
  • Using the new FOI regulations proposed under the forthcoming Charter for Social Housing white paper to build the register.
