S106 Rent Checker

Make sure your housing association is not charging you too much rent

Enter Your Postcode

The Problem

Housing associations are charging higher rents than they should be for what should be social rented homes - in some cases up to 80% of market rent.

The Project

The S106 Tenure Checker is a first step towards making housing associations in Southwark operate in a fairer and more transparent way for tenants and charge the social rents agreed at planning approval or by funding agreements. There are three parts to the Checker:

  • The register - a public register of all the (xxx,000) social rented homes secured via section 106 agreements in the past ten years.
  • A lookup tool so that tenants can look up their address, find out if their home is subject to a section 106 agreement and discover if the rent charged by the housing association is higher than it should be.
  • Reporting tool â€“ this is a facility to enable tenants to make a complaint about the housing association to their local authority/GLA if the rent charged is higher than the social rent levels.

How does this Checker comply with the GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018?

Information about affordable homes secured through the planning process or funded by the Mayor does not constitute personal data and is therefore not subject to the General Data Protection Regulation 2016/679 (GDPR) or the Data Protection Act 2018. The information is also published elsewhere i.e. in planning documents (accommodation schedules) and the Land Registry.

Why the need for this tool?

A recent government report confirms that "There is very little data published that explicitly addresses affordable housing stock, particularly split out by tenure" and that current data is based on affordable homes secured via planning approvals or GLA funding rather than what has actually been built.

"Supply data usually comes from the administrative systems of funding programmes, which are no longer concerned with the housing once it has been delivered."

Why are local authorities and the Mayor reluctant to act?

  • First, the current situation is convenient because they are able to claim that they have secured x number of new social rented homes and it would be politically embarrassing to acknowledge that they have been provided as a different tenure (or not at all)
  • Second, because of the nature of their reporting system, neither local authorities or the Mayor are actually able to monitor the tenure of affordable homes because they don't have a record of the addresses or access to them through the housing associations.
